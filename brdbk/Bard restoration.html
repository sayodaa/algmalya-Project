Restoration Techniques

Based on the current condition of the monument and the issues identified through field surveys and chronological analysis (time line), these deteriorations can be categorized into three principal groups: structural problems, minor material decay, and human-induced damage. Each category requires specific conservation interventions, employing distinct methodologies in line with international standards of heritage safeguarding.

1. Structural Problems

Description and Location
The structural pathologies are predominantly observed in the main façade, particularly above the entrance and beneath the minaret, where a progressive and continuous vertical crack has developed. Addressing this pathology necessitates the application of advanced structural conservation techniques, such as:

  1. Syntac Technique
    Recognized as one of the modern interventions in crack consolidation, this method involves creating cavities along both sides of the crack at the middle masonry layer, filling them with epoxy resin, and subsequently inserting stainless steel or iron dowels. This process provides effective mechanical bonding between the separated elements, enhancing cohesion and restoring the structural integrity of the historic fabric.
  2. Stitching Technique
    A structural consolidation method used to tie and stabilize fractured masonry units, ensuring the preservation of the load-bearing capacity of the architectural element.
  3. Dismantling and Reassembly
    Recommended in areas with severely deteriorated stones, ensuring the proper interlocking (toothing) between newly integrated blocks and the original masonry, in order to achieve long-term structural stability and material compatibility.

Preliminary Stabilization Measures
Before commencing crack treatment, soil grouting beneath the minaret is recommended as a preventive measure to mitigate ongoing settlement, thereby safeguarding the monument’s overall stability.

2. Minor Material Decay

Types and Characteristics
Black Crusts: Treated using conservation poultices tailored to the substrate and encrustation type, with repeated applications to ensure full removal, following conservation ethics of minimal intervention.

Salt Efflorescence: Requires careful desalination strategies beginning with mechanical cleaning (e.g., soft brushes and micro-tools), supported by selected poultices, while avoiding any loss of original fabric.

Surface Dust and Deposits: Removed through gentle mechanical cleaning techniques, ensuring the protection of the historic material and patina.

3. Human-Induced Damage

Nature and Impact
Anthropogenic deterioration is among the most critical threats to cultural heritage. Mitigation requires the integration of preventive conservation strategies through community engagement programs, including awareness campaigns, workshops, and training activities. These initiatives highlight the cultural and historical value of the monument, strengthening its role in identity building and contributing to the broader objectives of sustainable heritage management and long-term safeguarding of cultural assets.
